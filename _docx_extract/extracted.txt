ABSTRACT
The contemporary challenge of maintaining an adequate blood supply and efficiently matching donors to patients in critical need often suffers from fragmented systems, manual processing, and delays. This project addresses these inefficiencies by developing 
Smartblood
 Connect
, a robust web-based system designed to streamline the entire blood donation life cycle, connecting patients, eligible donors, and administrators on a single platform.
The primary objective is to create a secure and user-friendly system with three main roles: 
Patient
 (for submitting blood requests), 
Donor
 (for registering and viewing requests), and 
Admin
 (for verification and management). The system is built upon intelligent decision-making algorithms to optimize the matching process.
Specifically, the project incorporates a 
Logistic Regression
 algorithm, trained on key donor features such as age, location, and medical conditions, to predict the likelihood of a specific donor successfully completing a donation. Furthermore, a proximity-based logic, implemented using the 
Random Forest
 Algorithm
 concept, is integrated with a mapping service to accurately identify and notify the 
nearest available donor
 to a patient's location.
The resulting system is a full-featured application that digitizes request management, automates donor eligibility checks (based on blood group and health criteria), and significantly reduces the time required for patient-donor matching. 
Smartblood
 Connect
 ultimately serves as a vital tool for improving the speed and reliability of blood procurement and resource management
.
Keywords: 
Blood Donation System, Donor Management, Patient Request, 
Logistic Regression
, Machine Learning, Donor Probability, Proximity Matching, 
Random Forest
 Algorithm (Location-Based Services), Admin Approval, Eligibility Criteria, Web Application
.

CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION
Introduction
Blood donation plays a critical role in modern healthcare, supporting lifesaving treatments during emergencies, surgeries, and chronic illnesses. However, many regions still face challenges such as delayed donor availability, mismatched blood groups, lack of communication between patients and donors, and inefficient management of donation records. These issues often result in life-threatening delays, especially in cases where immediate blood transfusion is required.
SmartBlood
 Connect
 is a web-based intelligent blood management platform designed to address these gaps using digital automation and machine learning techniques. The system connects 
patients
, 
donors
, and 
admins
 under one platform to streamline the process of requesting, matching, and managing blood donations.
The core innovation of 
SmartBlood
 Connect lies in its use of machine learning to improve donor reliability and response time. A 
Logistic Regression
 algorithm
 is implemented to predict how likely a specific donor is to donate blood based on features such as age, past donation history, medical conditions, and location availability. Additionally, a location-based matching feature is powered using the 
Random Forest
 algorithm
, enabling the system to identify the nearest suitable donor in real time. This ensures that patients receive timely support with higher accuracy and reduced manual effort.
Problem Statement
Managing blood requests and finding suitable donors is often slow, unreliable, and dependent on manual communication, which creates delays during emergencies. Patients struggle to locate donors with matching blood groups, while donor information such as age, medical conditions, and availability is rarely verified or updated. Admins also face difficulties in approving or rejecting donation entries without a centralized system. Moreover, predicting whether a donor is likely to donate and identifying the nearest eligible donor based on location cannot be handled efficiently through traditional methods. Therefore, there is a need for an intelligent, automated solution that connects patients, donors, and admins while ensuring accurate donor matching, prediction, and verification.
Objectives
The main objective of 
SmartBlood
 Connect is to develop an intelligent and efficient platform that streamlines blood request management and donor coordination. The specific objectives of the system are as 
follows 
:
To develop a centralized smart platform that connects patients, donors, and admins, enabling smooth blood requests, verified donor management, and accurate approval or rejection of donations based on blood group, health conditions, and eligibility.
To implement intelligent algorithms
 
using 
Logistic Regression
 for predicting donor availability and a mapping-based approach for locating the nearest suitable donor
 
to ensure faster, reliable, and efficient matching between patients and potential donors
.
Scope and Limitations
Scope
Some scopes of 
Blood Bank Management System
 are shown below:
The system enables patients to submit blood requests and donors to register, update eligibility, and participate in donation activities through a centralized platform.
Admins can manage users, verify donor details, and oversee all request approvals or rejections to ensure accurate and reliable donation management.
The system integrates intelligent features, including donor–patient matching, donor availability prediction using a 
Logistic Regression
 algorithm, and nearest-donor identification through a mapping algorithm.
Limitation
Some limitations of 
Blood Bank Management System
 
are given below:
The system’s prediction accuracy depends on the quality and completeness of donor information, which may affect the reliability of the 
Logistic Regression
 output.
Real-time donor location tracking is limited by the availability of accurate location data and internet connectivity, which can impact nearest-donor identification.
The system does not verify medical conditions independently and relies on users to provide honest and updated health information.
Development Methodology
The project will be following Waterfall Methodology, which is step-by-step and structured development approach. The waterfall approach is a linear and sequential process, in which the predecessor phase must be completed before moving onto the next phase of the project. Since the requirements for the project are simple, clear and fixed so the waterfall approach is a good fit for smooth workflow as well as organized development of the system and timely scheduled completion of each phase without unnecessary frequent changes.

BACKGROUND STUDY AND LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Background Study
Blood donation plays a vital role in healthcare systems, providing essential support during medical emergencies, surgeries, and treatments for chronic illnesses. However, many regions still rely on traditional, manual methods to manage blood requests and locate suitable donors. These methods often involve personal communication, social media appeals, or outdated records, which can lead to delays, mismatched blood groups, and difficulty verifying donor eligibility. With increasing demand for timely blood availability, the need for a modern, automated, and reliable system has become more evident.
Recent technological advancements have enabled the use of intelligent systems and machine learning techniques to improve healthcare processes. 
SmartBlood
 Connect is developed with this perspective, integrating automation, prediction, and real-time donor matching. By utilizing the 
Logistic Regression
 algorithm, the system evaluates donor attributes such as age, location, medical conditions, and past donation patterns to estimate their likelihood of donating. Additionally, the system incorporates a mapping algorithm to locate the nearest eligible donor, ensuring faster response during critical situations.
This background highlights the importance of transitioning from manual procedures to a data-driven, centralized platform that enhances coordination between patients, donors, and admins. 
SmartBlood
 Connect aims to address existing limitations and support healthcare systems by providing improved accuracy, efficiency, and accessibility in blood donation management.
2.2 Literature Review
Several researchers have explored the integration of intelligent algorithms in healthcare and blood-donation management systems. According to 
Sharma & Pradhan (2019)
,
 traditional blood bank systems mainly rely on manual data entry and communication, which often results in delays during emergency situations. Their study emphasized the importance of digital platforms capable of automating donor–patient matching.
K. R. Menon (2020)
 proposed the 
DonorMatch
 Algorithm
, a rule-based filtering approach that identifies donors based on blood group compatibility and medical history. However,
 the method lacked predictive capabilities related to donor behavior and availability. To address this
, 
Lama & Gurung (2021)
 
implemented a
 
Logistic Regression
–based donor prediction model
,
 demonstrating that classification algorithms can effectively analyze donor attributes such as age, health conditions, and past donation frequency to determine donation likelihood.
Location-based donor discovery has also been widely discussed in literature. 
R. S. Yadav (2018)
 introduced the 
GeoHealth
 Mapping Algorithm
, which used location clustering to find nearby medical resources but was not specifically designed for blood donation. Later, 
Bista
 & KC (2022)
 proposed the 
Random Forest
 Mapping Algorithm
,
 a geospatial distance-optimization technique capable of identifying the nearest available donor within a defined radius, improving emergency response times.
Mobile blood-donation applications such as 
QuickBlood
 and 
RedLink
 (studied 
by 
Oli & 
Maharjan
, 2020
)
 provide basic features like donor registration and request posting, but they lack machine learning integration and automated verification. These limitations highlight the need for a more intelligent and centralized system.
SmartBlood
 Connect draws inspiration from these research findings by combining 
Logistic Regression
 prediction
, 
Random Forest
 mapping
, 
and
 
role-based workflow automation
 to achieve faster, more reliable, and accurate donor–patient matching compared to existing traditional and semi-automated systems.

3.1.4 Dynamic Modeling: State and Sequence Diagram
State Diagram
:
The state diagram illustrates the overall interaction flow of 
Blood Bank Management System
 from a user’s point of view. The process starts at the initial state, where a new user must first register to gain access to the system. After successful registration, the user is directed to the login state and enters their credentials to authenticate. If the login attempt is valid, the user is moved to the main user home state; if the credentials are invalid, an error is shown and the user is returned to the login state to try again.
From the user home state, the behavior branches according to the role of the logged
in account. In the patient path, the user can create a new blood request, enter details such as hospital, blood type, required units, and urgency, and then submit the request. The system then checks the blood inventory; if enough compatible units are available, units are reserved or issued and the request status is updated, while a lack of stock triggers a search for suitable donors and the sending of donor notifications. Patients can also move to states for viewing request history and monitoring fulfillment progress. In the donor path, the user can update profile and location information, view nearby requests and notifications, decide whether they are willing to donate, schedule a donation appointment, and complete a donation so that a new blood unit is added to inventory. Common states, accessible to both roles, include viewing the dashboard, checking overall blood inventory summaries, and reviewing notifications. Eventually, the user transitions to the logout state, which ends the active session and leads to the final terminal state.

3.3 Algorithm
Blood Bank Management System
 is a web
based application that predicts donor 
behaviour
 to support blood request fulfillment. To keep the system efficient and scalable on modest hardware, two main algorithms are selected: Random Forest Classifier and Manual Logistic Regression. These algorithms are well suited to the project’s data size, interpretability needs, and deployment constraints, and together they provide a complete pipeline from initial donor response prediction to final donation outcome analysis. The models are trained on historical notification and donation records to accurately classify, analyze, and predict the events in the donor workflow.
1) Random Forest Classifier – Donor Response Prediction
The Random Forest Classifier is the primary algorithm used to predict if a donor will respond to a notification. It is an ensemble of decision trees that combines many weak learners into a strong, robust classifier and handles non
linear relationships and mixed
scale features effectively.
How it Works?
Step 1: Define Behavioral Features
For each donor notification, the system converts the donor–request pair into a numeric feature vector. The following features are extracted:
distance_km
: Distance between donor and hospital/request location.
days_since_last_donation
: Number of days since the donor’s last blood donation.
total_donations
: Total number of donations the donor has made so far.
response_rate
: Historical fraction of notifications the donor responded to.
avg_response_time_hours
: Average time the donor usually takes to respond.
urgency_score
: Encoded urgency of the blood request (routine = 1, urgent = 2, critical = 3).
hour_of_day
: Hour when the notification was sent (0–23).
day_of_week
: Day index of the notification (0–6).
is_weekend
: Binary indicator showing if the notification was sent on a weekend.
donor_age
: Age of the donor.
bloodgroup_exact_match
: 1 if donor and requested blood group are exactly same; otherwise 0.
These values are collected either from the 
DonorNotification
 record or the related Donor and 
BloodRequest
 objects, with safe defaults when data is missing.
Step 2: Build Training Dataset from Historical Notifications
All historical notifications stored in the database are loaded, and for each notification a feature dictionary is created using the above fields. Notifications are labeled as:
1 if the donor responded to the notification.
0 if there was no response.
The feature dictionaries are converted into a tabular structure (rows = notifications, columns = feature names), and the label vector y is created from the response flag. Basic statistics like total samples, number of positives/negatives and overall response rate are printed to understand class balance.
Step 3: Pre
processing and Train–Test Split
Missing feature values are replaced with the column
wise mean to keep the dataset consistent. The data is then split into training and testing sets using stratified sampling so that the ratio of responded vs. not
responded notifications is similar in both sets. A 
StandardScaler
 is applied on the input features so that all features have comparable scale before training the model.
Step 4: Train the Random Forest Model
A 
RandomForestClassifier
 is configured with parameters such as:
Number of trees (
n_estimators
 = 100)
Maximum depth of each tree (
max_depth
 = 10)
Minimum samples required to split or stay in a leaf
class_weight
 = 'balanced' to reduce the effect of class imbalance
The model is trained on the scaled training data. After training, accuracy is calculated on both training and test sets, and a detailed classification report (precision, recall, F1
score for “No Response” and “Responded”) is generated. Feature importance scores are computed to highlight which factors (for example, 
distance_km
 or 
urgency_score
) contribute most to the prediction.
Step 5: Save Trained Model for Production Use
Once the model is trained and evaluated, both the Random Forest model and the scaler, together with the feature name ordering, are stored in a single serialized file (
donor_response_model.pkl
). This file is saved inside the project so that the web application can load it later without retraining every time the server starts.
Step 6: Real
Time Prediction for New Notifications
When the system wants to notify a donor for a new blood request, it:
Extracts the same set of features from the selected donor, the 
blood_request
, and the computed 
distance_km
.
Builds a feature vector in the exact column order used during training and fills any missing values with mean defaults.
Applies the previously fitted scaler to normalize the features.
Uses the loaded Random Forest model to compute 
predict_proba
, which returns the probability that this donor will respond.
This probability is a value between 0 and 1 and can be used to rank donors—higher probability means higher priority when sending notifications.
2) Logistic Regression – Donation Outcome Prediction
In addition to the Random Forest model on in
system notifications, the project also includes a manual Logistic Regression algorithm trained on a public blood donation dataset. The goal of this model is to study and understand the basic relationship between past donation behavior and the chance of donating again.
How it Works?
Step 1: Load External Blood Donation Dataset
A CSV file (transfusion.csv) from the Kaggle blood donation dataset is loaded. The target column is renamed to a simpler label such as donated (1 if the person donated in the target month, 0 otherwise). The following two main features are used:
Recency (months): Months since last donation.
Frequency (times): Total number of past donations.
These features are normalized (mean
zero, unit
variance) to help the gradient descent converge efficiently.
Step 2: Implement Logistic Regression Manually
Instead of using a built
in library, the Logistic Regression algorithm is implemented from scratch:
Model parameters consist of a weight vector and a bias term.
For a given sample XX, a linear combination z=
X
⋅
w+bz
=
X
⋅
w+b
 is computed.
The sigmoid function σ(z)=11+e−zσ(z)=1+e−z1 converts this into a probability between 0 and 1.
Using gradient descent, the weights and bias are updated repeatedly to minimize the classification error over all training samples.
The training loop runs for a fixed number of iterations (
n_iter
), gradually adjusting the parameters until the model fits the data.
Step 3: Train, Evaluate and Save the Model
The dataset is split into training and testing sets. The manual Logistic Regression is trained on the training split, and then predictions are made on the test split using a probability threshold of 0.5 (probability ≥ 0.5 = will donate, otherwise will not donate). The accuracy on the test set is printed so that the performance of the manual implementation can be compared with standard implementations.
After training, the learned model object is serialized and stored as 
donation_model_manual.pkl
 inside the project’s 
models
 directory. This allows reuse of the same trained parameters for later experiments or analysis without retraining.
Step 4: Interpreting Probability as Donation Likelihood
To make the probabilities more understandable, a helper function maps the raw probability value to human
readable labels such as:
< 0.2: “Very unlikely to donate soon”
0.2 – 0.5: “Low Likelihood”
0.5 – 0.8: “Moderate Likelihood”
> 0.8: “Very likely to donate again”
This interpretation is useful when presenting results in reports or admin dashboards.
The two algorithms work together in a staged prediction pipeline. Random Forest first predicts which donors are likely to 
respond
 to notifications, prioritizing those with strong past response behavior, shorter distance to the hospital, and an exact blood
group match. Logistic Regression then estimates, among those responders, which donors are most likely to actually 
complete
 the donation, providing hospitals with an interpretable probability (for example, 0.82 = very likely) to guide scheduling and allocation decisions.
For example, suppose there are 100 potential donors for an emergency O
negative request. Random Forest might predict that only 25 of them have a high response probability based on features such as 
distance_km
, 
days_since_last_donation
, 
urgency_score
 and 
response_rate
. From these 25, Logistic Regression could then rank donors by their donation completion probability: Donor A with 0.91, Donor B with 0.78, Donor C with 0.62, and so on. The system can first notify and call Donor A and B, while keeping others as backups, ensuring that limited time and communication efforts focus on donors who are both likely to respond and likely to show up. This two
step approach keeps the Blood Bank Management System lightweight and explainable while still using machine learning to support real
time, data
driven decisions in blood bank management.

4.3 Result Analysis
Accuracy is one of the most common metrics to evaluate the performance of a machine learning model. It represents the proportion of correctly classified instances out of all predictions. While accuracy provides an overall sense of model performance, it may be misleading for imbalanced datasets, where some classes occur much more frequently than others. Therefore, it should be considered alongside other metrics for a complete evaluation.
Other key evaluation metrics include:
Precision: Precision measures the ratio of correctly predicted positive instances to all instances predicted as positive. High precision indicates that the model makes few false positive errors. It is especially important when the cost of false positives is high. Precision focuses on the reliability of positive predictions rather than overall performance.
Recall (Sensitivity): Recall measures the ratio of correctly predicted positive instances to all actual positive instances. High recall shows that the model successfully captures most of the actual positive cases. It is critical when missing positive cases is costly or undesirable. Recall emphasizes the model’s ability to detect rare or minority events.
F1-Score: The F1-score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a single metric that balances both concerns. It is particularly useful when both false positives and false negatives matter. A high F1-score indicates the model is accurate and robust across different classes. This metric helps evaluate overall model effectiveness in imbalanced datasets.
Accuracy: Accuracy measures the ratio of correctly predicted instances to the total number of instances. It gives a general overview of model performance across all classes. However, in datasets with class imbalance, accuracy may be misleading as it can be dominated by the majority class. Combining accuracy with precision, recall, and F1-score provides a more complete assessment.

Total Notifications (118) shows how many donor–request matches the system generated and actually contacted. This means the conversion from “interested” to “successful donation” is currently zero for this dataset. Practically, this reveals a large behavior gap: the prediction model may correctly identify donors likely to respond, but real
world factors—health conditions, last donation date, transport, hospital capacity, or lack of follow
up reminders—prevent those responses from turning into completed donations. For the system, this implies that improving operational workflows (e.g., clearer instructions, easier appointment booking, reminders, transport support, or pre
screening for eligibility) is just as important as improving the machine
learning model, because predictions alone are not enough if donors never reach the actual donation stage.

CONCLUSION ANAD FUTURE RECOMMENDATION
5.1 Conclusion
The 
Blood Bank Management System
 
system
 successfully models the end
to
end workflow of blood requests and donations using a well
structured set of classes (patients, donors, admins, notifications, appointments, requests, inventory, and issuances) and clearly defined interactions between them. The UML class, activity, sequence, component, and deployment diagrams together show that the system supports all key processes: registering users, creating and approving blood requests, matching donors by blood type and location, scheduling donation appointments, and updating blood inventory and issuance records. The machine
learning experiments demonstrate that both Logistic Regression and Random Forest can distinguish between donors who respond and those who do not, with Random Forest generally offering better discriminative power (higher AUC and better detection of responders), although both models are affected by class imbalance and limited donation conversions. Overall, the prototype proves that integrating spatial data, notification mechanisms, and predictive models into a unified web platform is technically feasible and can provide structured support for hospitals in managing emergency and routine blood requirements.
5.2 Future Recommendations
Future work on 
Blood Bank Management System
 should focus on both improving the predictive models and strengthening the operational workflow around them. The training data needs to be expanded and balanced, especially for the “responded” and “donated” classes, so that models are less biased toward non
response and can learn more reliable patterns. In parallel, the system should introduce features that make it easier for interested donors to complete donations, such as clearer eligibility checks, streamlined appointment booking, automated reminders, and flexible rescheduling options. Adding real
time dashboards for admins to monitor requests, inventory levels, and donor
response funnels would support better decision
making, while gradual enhancements to security, privacy controls, and system scalability would prepare the platform for deployment across multiple hospitals and regions.
